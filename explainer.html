ViennaPS / via-middle TSV process / HBM4 case study

Using ViennaPS to research a complete TSV traveler

A practical tour of the ViennaPS-HBM repository, followed by a controlled case study of patterning, Bosch etch, liner, barrier and seed, Cu fill, and CMP. Every chart uses sampled simulation data. Failed hypotheses and model limits remain visible.

Scope: single-feature topography (etch, deposition, CMP), not package warpage, bonding thermal/mechanical reliability, or JEDEC signaling. Not a fab-calibrated DOE. This is a sensitivity study on simulation model coefficients, no measured process data to validate against.

OVERVIEW

What this package demonstrates

ViennaPS can connect recipe inputs to evolving TSV geometry and expose failure modes across a full traveler. In the audited wired model space, pattern through barrier can pass reliably; Cu fill and CMP remain model-limited misses.

The value of the case study is the method: define targets, screen knobs, control stochastic geometry, test interactions, expand boundaries, confirm on unseen seeds, and state model ceilings narrowly.

METHOD

How the autoresearch DOE was designed

The campaign was staged so each experiment answered the question created by the previous one. Scalar loss never overruled invalid metrics, CMP mask loss, target feasibility, or replicated tail behavior.

1. AuditInspect every wired knob, API control, target, and scoring rule.
2. ScreenUse broad 96 to 160 recipe blocks with four seeds to locate credible regions.
3. ControlReuse each stochastic Bosch geometry across downstream arms.
4. ChallengeTest interactions, expand boundaries, and preserve negative results.
5. ConfirmUse sixteen unseen seeds, then perturb finalists to map robustness.

Select a generation point to read the decision. The managed metric is lower-is-better, but a lower value never overrides invalid metrics, mask loss, target infeasibility, or weak replication.

LEARNINGS

What a full-traveler simulation can teach us

Geometry carries forwardA small upstream profile change can alter every downstream coverage and fill result.
Noise can imitate interactionDownstream arms must share the same stochastic Bosch geometry before effects are compared.
Gates change the optimumLower dish is worthless when the CMP mask is consumed; coverage cannot replace a zero-gap target.
Boundaries are hypothesesA winning edge point must be expanded before it can be called an optimum.
Failure can locate missing physicsPersistent, replicated morphology can show where a model form needs CEAC or pad-pressure physics.

Practical use: this type of study narrows physical experiments, identifies sensitive recipe families, exposes coupled failure modes, and prevents confidence in a recipe that only wins through a scoring artifact. It cannot replace calibration data or validate mechanisms that the model does not represent.

HISTORY

The precursor studies and the mistakes they exposed

These earlier etch and fill studies established the repo's first response surfaces and several durable methodological rules. They are kept here as research history. The final full-traveler campaign above is the decision authority.

01

Precursor study: screening beyond the first four etch factors

A 768-run DOE across 4 etch parameters found etch_time dominant. But bosch_etch exposes 11 parameters in this wrapper. The other seven had only ever sat at their defaults. Screening all 7 (one-factor-at- a-time from the current best point, low/high vs. baseline) found two, initial_etch_time and neutral_rate, that rank above two of the original 4. confirmed

Baseline bulge . Each bar is the larger of the two deltas from baseline (low value versus high value tested). This is a screen, not a full sweep, so it tells you which knobs are worth a combined DOE, not the exact optimum.

Compare against the original 4-parameter DOE this screening was checking against:

Confound caught in the raw sweep: its top result looked like a ~3.7x improvement. Short etch_time at a fixed cycle count means a shallower via, and shallower vias trivially show less wall bulge. That is a measurement artifact, not a process improvement. Depth-matched, replicated (3x, Monte Carlo ray-traced runs have real run-to-run noise): a genuine 2.8x improvement survives, not 3.7x.

02

Precursor study: combined etch DOE and a corrected ranking

800 runs across the new top-4 (etch_time x neutral_sticking_probability x initial_etch_time x neutral_rate). Result: neutral_rate's effect is nearly as large as etch_time's. The screening ranking had it below initial_etch_time, which turns out to be the smallest effect of the four. screening ranking overturned

The earlier etch reference, depth-matched (14 cycles versus 12) and replicated three times, showed about 1.5x lower wall bulge than its predecessor. This became the precursor traveler reference. The study also checked etch_time=0.5 wasn't just an untested boundary: swept below it (0.2 to 0.4) and bulge gets monotonically worse. This is a genuine optimum, not a cutoff artifact.

Subconformal (faster at the opening) traps a void. Superconformal (directional, bottom-up) reaches the floor, but a residual gap remains at the tip at every setting tested. tested model-space ceiling

The sharp via tip has less flux view-factor in this deposition model. More thickness alone does not close it. Real superfill closes this via curvature-dependent accelerator chemistry (CEAC); a constant-direction deposition model structurally can't represent that mechanism.

07

CMP: the "over-polish causes dishing" claim was backwards

Checked against an actual field-vs-via measurement instead of assumed. The textbook-correct, nominal overburden leaves severe dishing; fixing it needs enough extra material removal to fully consume the mask layer. claim reversed after measurement

ps.IsotropicProcess removes material at the same rate everywhere regardless of local topography. It has no concept of polish-pad contact pressure, the mechanism that lets real CMP preferentially clear raised regions. The result is a ceiling of this isotropic-removal wrapper, not a physical CMP conclusion.

08

Patterning's mask taper: a claim made, then retracted

Checking all 5 steps' parameters jointly (not each step against only its own metric) is how a cross-stage interaction would surface. It looked like one did for maskTaperAngle. With one run each, taper=0 winning on bulge, taper=4 on fill tip-gap. Replicating properly (6-10 runs, not 1) says otherwise. retracted after replication

Plainly: we thought tilting the mask sidewall by 4 degrees traded a straighter etch for a better Cu fill. It doesn't. The etch part is real: it makes the etch more repeatable, not more likely to hit a specific number. The fill part isn't real at all; we can't tell taper=0 and taper=4 apart on fill quality once you run it more than once.

What's real: taper=4 gives a near-zero-variance bulge every run; taper=0 (a perfectly vertical mask sidewall) gives a genuinely bimodal distribution. A numerical-stability difference, not a "better via" finding. The tip-gap ranges overlap almost completely, no basis for that half of the original claim.
