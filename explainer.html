Bosch etch & Cu fill: what actually controls the outcome

HBM stacks depend on TSVs -- microscopic vertical tunnels etched through silicon and filled with copper to connect stacked memory dies. Three things have to be right: the etch must be straight (not bowed or scalloped), it must reach a target depth (too shallow and it never connects through the die; too deep wastes process time), and the copper fill must be void-free (a trapped air pocket cracks the chip later). This is a data-driven search for what actually controls those three outcomes, using ViennaPS to simulate the real process physics.

Bottom line

A 768-run, 4-parameter DOE found that etch_time (per-cycle duration) — not the parameter most people would guess first — is the dominant control on TSV wall straightness. Screening all 11 of the etch step's real parameters (not just the 4 already being varied) found two more that matter; a second, 800-run combined DOE on the new top-4 found a recipe ~1.5x better than the original DOE's own winner, depth- matched and replicated before being trusted.

Every step's real parameters were screened this way, then checked jointly across all 5 process steps -- not just optimized one at a time -- specifically to catch a knob that helps one stage while hurting another. One looked real at first (patterning's mask taper angle) and then didn't survive replication: reported below as a retraction, not smoothed over. CMP's dishing behavior was also found to be the opposite of what an earlier pass assumed -- also corrected below with real measurements.

Scope: this models etch/deposition topography at single-feature scale. It does not model package warpage, TCB/hybrid-bonding thermal reliability, or JEDEC signaling — see the notebook README for the full picture.

768-run DOE: which parameter actually matters

4 parameters (ion_source_exponent, neutral_sticking_probability, etch_time, deposition_thickness) at 8x8x4x3 = 768 combinations. etch_time turned out to dominate -- bigger than the isotropic component a smaller 2-parameter sweep had suggested was the main knob.

Methodology note: the raw sweep's top result looked dramatically better than it really was -- short etch_time at a fixed cycle count means a shallower via, and shallower vias trivially show less wall bulge regardless of whether the recipe is actually better. Verified by regenerating the winner at a depth-matched cycle count across 3 replicates (needed because these are Monte Carlo ray-traced runs with real run-to-run noise): a genuine ~2.8x improvement survives the correction, just not the ~3.7x the confounded raw number implied.

Screening pass: 2 more real knobs, an 800-run follow-up DOE

bosch_etch exposes 11 real parameters, not 4 -- the other 7 had only ever been left at their defaults. Screening all 7 found initial_etch_time and neutral_rate ranked above two of the original DOE's own parameters. This chart is the follow-up: a combined 800-run DOE on the new top-4.

A ranking reversal, caught rather than assumed: the earlier screening ranked initial_etch_time above neutral_rate. This full combined DOE found the opposite -- neutral_rate's effect is nearly as large as etch_time's, while initial_etch_time turned out to be the smallest of the four. Screening from one baseline point can mis-rank parameters that interact; the combined DOE is what determines the real ranking. This new winner, depth- matched and replicated against the previous production recipe, is ~1.5x better and is now the recipe used throughout.

CMP: a claim found backwards, then corrected

An earlier pass claimed "over-polishing causes dishing, stopping at the exact nominal target plane fixes it." Checked against an actual field-vs-via measurement rather than assumed -- it's backwards.

The nominal, textbook-correct overburden leaves severe dishing. Removing more material does reduce it, but the mask layer is fully consumed by the time dishing drops to a value any real fab would accept -- destroying real device structure well before the numbers look good. ps.IsotropicProcess removes material at the same rate everywhere regardless of local topography; it has no concept of the polish-pad contact pressure that's the actual mechanism letting real CMP planarize. A genuine structural ceiling of this simple process model, documented rather than hidden.

Patterning's mask taper: a claim made, checked, and retracted

Checking all 5 process steps' real parameters jointly (not each step optimized only against its own metric) is how a cross-stage interaction would show up if one existed. `maskTaperAngle` looked like exactly that -- until replication showed otherwise.

Retracted after replication. A first pass, checked with one run per taper value, claimed taper=0 wins on etch bulge while taper=4 wins on Cu-fill tip-gap -- a real trade-off. Replicating properly (6-10 runs instead of 1) shows the tip-gap ranges for the two candidates almost completely overlap: no statistical basis for that half of the claim. What IS real: taper=4 gives an essentially deterministic bulge result every run, while taper=0 (a perfectly vertical mask sidewall) produces a genuinely bimodal distribution -- usually similar, occasionally much better. A real numerical-stability difference, not a "better via" finding. Reported this way because the tidier trade-off story was wrong.
