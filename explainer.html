Bosch etch + Cu fill: a DOE findings log, not a highlight reel

HBM stacks depend on TSVs: micron-scale vertical tunnels etched through silicon and filled with copper to connect stacked dies. This is a running log of what was screened, what was found, and what was retracted after replication didn't support it -- in the order it actually happened.

Scope: single-feature topography (etch, deposition, CMP), not package warpage, bonding thermal/mechanical reliability, or JEDEC signaling. Not a fab-calibrated DOE -- a sensitivity study on simulation model coefficients, no measured process data to validate against.

01

Screening the full parameter set, not just the 4 already varied

A 768-run DOE across 4 etch parameters found etch_time dominant. But bosch_etch exposes 11 real parameters -- the other 7 had only ever sat at their defaults. Screening all 7 found two, initial_etch_time and neutral_rate, that rank above two of the original 4. confirmed

Confound caught in the raw sweep: its top result looked like a ~3.7x improvement. Short etch_time at a fixed cycle count means a shallower via, and shallower vias trivially show less wall bulge -- not a real improvement, a measurement artifact. Depth-matched, replicated (3x, Monte Carlo ray-traced runs have real run-to-run noise): a genuine 2.8x improvement survives, not 3.7x.

02

The screened knobs, combined-DOE'd and verified

800 runs across the new top-4 (etch_time x neutral_sticking_probability x initial_etch_time x neutral_rate). Result: neutral_rate's effect is nearly as large as etch_time's -- screening's own ranking had it below initial_etch_time, which turns out to be the smallest effect of the four. screening ranking overturned

New winner, depth-matched (14 cycles vs. the old recipe's 12) and replicated (3x) against the previous production recipe: ~1.5x better wall bulge. Adopted as production. Also checked etch_time=0.5 wasn't just an untested boundary: swept below it (0.2-0.4) and bulge gets monotonically worse -- a genuine optimum, not a cutoff artifact.

05

Cu fill: three growth regimes, one unclosed ceiling

Subconformal (faster at the opening) traps a void. Superconformal (directional, bottom-up) reaches the floor -- but a residual gap remains at the tip at every setting tested. structural ceiling, not a tuning gap

SUBCONFORMAL

faster at opening → pinches shut

SUPERCONFORMAL

faster at floor, swept-optimal

The sharp via tip has less flux view-factor for any deposition model -- more thickness alone doesn't close it. Real superfill closes this via curvature-dependent accelerator chemistry (CEAC); a constant-direction deposition model structurally can't represent that mechanism.

07

CMP: the "over-polish causes dishing" claim was backwards

Checked against an actual field-vs-via measurement instead of assumed. The textbook-correct, nominal overburden leaves severe dishing; fixing it needs enough extra material removal to fully consume the mask layer. claim reversed after measurement

ps.IsotropicProcess removes material at the same rate everywhere regardless of local topography -- it has no concept of polish-pad contact pressure, the actual mechanism that lets real CMP preferentially clear raised regions. A structural ceiling of this simple model, not a recipe left untuned.

08

Patterning's mask taper: a claim made, then retracted

Checking all 5 steps' parameters jointly (not each step against only its own metric) is how a cross-stage interaction would surface. It looked like one did for maskTaperAngle -- one run each, taper=0 winning on bulge, taper=4 on fill tip-gap. Replicating properly (6-10 runs, not 1) says otherwise. retracted after replication

What's real: taper=4 gives a near-zero-variance bulge every run; taper=0 (a perfectly vertical mask sidewall) gives a genuinely bimodal distribution. A numerical-stability difference, not a "better via" finding -- the tip-gap ranges overlap almost completely, no basis for that half of the original claim.
