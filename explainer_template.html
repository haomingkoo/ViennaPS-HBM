ViennaPS teaching example · updated 17 Jul 2026

A simplified TSV traveler

This guide uses one simplified TSV flow to show how ViennaPS carries geometry between steps. It also shows how failures appear and how measurements turn a set of simulations into a trustworthy study.

Model the handoffsCarry one 2D geometry from mask opening through etch and films. Then add copper and polish.
Recognize failuresSee bowed walls and thin coatings. Find broken seed, trapped voids, and recessed plugs.
Build it correctlyMeasure every handoff. Test passing and failing controls. Repeat noisy runs before tuning.

This is a teaching model. Real TSV flows include more films, cleaning steps, and process controls.

What we are modeling

A through-silicon via (TSV) is a vertical electrical connection through silicon. This model covers the steps that form its copper plug.

  1. 1. Open the mask
  2. 2. Etch the silicon
  3. 3. Coat the walls
  4. 4. Prepare for copper
  5. 5. Fill with copper
  6. 6. Polish the surface
Inside this modelThe temporary mask is removed after etching. The liner is deposited next.
Later in fabricationWafer thinning exposes the bottom of the via. Wiring then completes the connection.

PRESCRIBED PASSING SHAPE · MEASUREMENT CHECK

How the software recognizes a passing shape

This positive control produces continuous geometric layers. No void is detected in the 2D copper region. Planarization leaves a connected plug.

StepWhat happensWhy it matters
1. Open the maskA pattern marks where the hole will be made.Sets the via's position and width.
2. Etch the siliconA deep hole is cut into the silicon.Creates the via and shapes its walls.
3. Coat the wallsAn insulating liner covers the inside surface.Keeps copper electrically isolated from silicon.
4. Prepare for copperA barrier and copper seed layer are added.The barrier slows copper migration. The seed gives plating a conductive surface.
5. Fill with copperCopper fills the coated hole.Creates the vertical electrical path.
6. Polish the surfaceExtra copper above the via is removed.Leaves a flat surface and a copper plug.

These passing shapes test the geometry and measurements. They do not show that a physical TSV recipe will produce the same result.

How the software works

Each simulated step uses the same loop. The current geometry and one process model go in. The updated geometry moves to the next step.

The domain stores material boundaries as level sets. This example uses a 2D cross-section.

Choosing a faster simulation setting

A faster setting is useful only when it preserves the measurements and pass/fail decisions needed by the study.

Points are saved simulations from one research machine. Lines connect sampled settings on a log x-axis; they are not fitted response surfaces. This benchmark uses one coarse-grid Bosch recipe, so its turning points do not transfer automatically to deeper or narrower vias.

Why 250 rays was rejected

At the high neutral-sticking test, the two ray counts disagreed about the width requirement. The limit is a maximum width error of 0.060 model units.

SettingMaximum width errorWidth requirementResult
250 rays0.0742Above 0.060Fail
2,000 rays0.0564Within 0.060Pass

Both runs reached the depth and bow requirements. Width alone changed the overall decision. That one disagreement is enough to reject 250 rays for the broad search. Evidence: 250-ray row · 2,000-ray row · width requirement.

How 500 rays will be checked

1. Freeze casesInclude clear passes, clear failures, difficult shapes and cases close to each limit.
2. Change rays onlyUse the same physical inputs, random streams and stopping rule at 500 and 2,000 rays.
3. Compare measurementsCheck depth, top/middle/bottom width, bow and shape class.
4. Check boundariesConfirm that pass/fail limits do not move enough to change a decision.
5. Approve a useIf every check agrees, use 500 for broad search. Confirm finalists at 2,000.

Current status: 500 rays is promising but not approved. The earlier comparison changed more than ray count, so it cannot answer this question cleanly.

What matters: the two settings do not need identical decimals. They need the same major shape, failure mode, pass/fail result and useful tuning direction. Small differences are acceptable when they cannot change a decision.

How the parameter search will work

Spread outTry a small set across the allowed ranges.
MeasureRecord the real outputs and every failed requirement.
FocusKeep controls that move useful measurements or failure boundaries.
Learn locallyTry promising points and uncertain boundary points in small batches.
ConfirmStop when added runs cannot change the decision. Recheck finalists with the slower setting.

This is a measured search, not Adam. Adam expects reliable gradients and many cheap, smooth evaluations. This simulator has expensive runs, hard failures and shape changes.

Use compute where it matters: screen many settings cheaply, remove clear failures, then spend more rays and repeats on survivors. Once a passing and failing setting bracket a boundary, test their midpoint. Stop when another split cannot change the engineering decision.

Main controls and common failures

Geometry inputs define the starting shape. Model inputs control simulated surface motion. Neither is a calibrated machine recipe.

StepInputs used hereCommon failureWhat this model checks
MaskOpening width, height, and taper. A wider opening starts a wider via.The opening is too wide or too narrow.Opening shape and remaining mask
Silicon etchCycle count and duration. Protective-coating dose and particle direction. More time usually etches deeper.The via is too shallow or its walls bow.Depth and width profile
LinerDeposition amount and particle sticking. Lower sticking can carry particles deeper.The floor is too thin or the opening narrows.Thickness, continuity, and remaining opening
Barrier + seedDeposition amount and directional fraction. More directional arrival can weaken deep coverage.The layer becomes thin or disconnected.Thickness, connection, and remaining opening
Copper fillFloor-to-wall growth balance. More wall growth closes the mouth sooner.The mouth closes first and traps a void.Void shape and copper above the surface
PolishA chosen removal plane. More removal clears the top but can recess the plug.Copper remains on top or the plug is recessed.Surface clearance and plug connection

Numerical settings: grid spacing sets geometric resolution. Ray count controls Monte Carlo sampling noise. A random seed selects a reproducible ray sample. None is a fab knob.

Physical recipe knobs: gas flow and pressure; power and temperature; current and chemistry. These are not calibrated in this project.

Advanced: failure modes and tuning details

StepPotential failureTuning knobPossible downside

Mask opening

Potential failureAn opening that is too wide, too narrow, or tapered.
Tuning knobOpening width, mask height, and taper.
Possible downsideThe opening must expose enough silicon while protecting the surrounding surface.

Silicon etch

Potential failureWrong depth, bowed walls, or a narrow neck.
Tuning knobCycle count, etch time, protective coating, and particle direction.
Possible downsideFaster removal can deepen the via but make its walls harder to control.

Liner

Potential failureA thin floor coating or a narrowed opening.
Tuning knobDeposition amount and particle sticking.
Possible downsideMore coating improves thickness but leaves less room for copper.

Barrier + seed

Potential failureA thin or disconnected coating near the floor.
Tuning knobDeposition amount and particle direction.
Possible downsideBetter deep coverage can also narrow the copper opening.

Copper fill

Potential failureA center seam, trapped void, or early mouth closure.
Tuning knobThe balance between floor growth and wall growth.
Possible downsideMore wall growth closes the mouth sooner.

Polish

Potential failureCopper left on top or a recessed plug.
Tuning knobThe removal endpoint and material removal rate.
Possible downsideMore removal clears the surface but can cut into the plug.

FOUNDATION CHECK COMPLETE

Use the verified full-width setup before the broad Bosch screen

Observation
All 24 cases are independently valid. Pattern gates pass 24/24. Etch mask and width pass 24/24 and 23/24. Depth and bow both pass 22/24.
Interpretation
The quarter-width shortcut changes depth and top CD; the coarse grid changes six outputs. Mask loss is monotonic, but every tested erosion arm survives.
Process move
Keep the full-width fine grid and qualify the lower ray count. Extend erosion until failure appears. Save selected shapes natively.
MEASUREMENT QUALIFIED

The baseline films are continuous, but not functional

Observation
All 12 layer runs remain continuous and leave the aperture open. The baseline still passes 0/4 liner gates and 0/4 barrier/seed gates at the fine setting.
Interpretation
The liner has enough local thickness but poor deep coverage: lower-wall/field is 0.859 against a 0.995 target. Barrier plus seed is too thin and highly directional: stack minimum is 0.00480 against 0.012.
Process move
Screen lower liner sticking for deeper coverage. For barrier and seed, test more conformal transport and additional dose while protecting the Cu-fill aperture.
SCRIPTED FILL CONTROL

A prescribed control produces a void-free plug in this geometry

Observation
The candidate rate law pinches laterally and fails at both tested grids. A prescribed bottom-up control fills the same protected stack at both grids with positive overburden.
Interpretation
The checks can register a void-free control, but this does not establish physical fillability. The tested law starts with nearly equal floor and wall velocity; the geometric criterion calls for about 10× at the floor.
Process move
Establish bottom selectivity before sweeping total Cu thickness. More conformal growth alone closes the sidewalls and traps a void.
TESTED 2D EXPANSION STOPPED

Lower sticking helps, but the gain is flattening

Observation
At sticking 0.00625, all 24 new runs are valid. Results at 1,600 and 3,200 reflections are identical. Suppressor transport improves in all eight paired streams, but the combined gate still passes 0/8.
Interpretation
The worst flux gain is 0.0041, below the 0.0167 numerical envelope. Floor/wall growth reaches 1.004–1.011 versus required ratios of 10.46 and 25.10.
Process move
Run the same transport question in a cylindrical 3D via. If 3D also misses, replace or extend the growth law before spending compute on Cu morphology or a large recipe sweep.
CMP METRIC CHECKED ON CONTROLS

The CMP metric distinguishes connected from cut layers in the tested controls

Observation
The new metric checks whether each material region connects the via floor to both mouth sides. Controlled planarization preserves the SiO2 stop and barrier at two grids. Seed and Cu also remain intact.
Interpretation
We can distinguish a connected layer from a cut layer. The removal model is still height and material dependent only; it has no pad-pressure physics.
Process move
Declare allowable stop and Cu-plug loss. Then screen material selectivity, endpoint, and overpolish. Do not optimize surface recess alone.

EXPERIMENT LOGIC

Why the large DOE is waiting

A broad response surface is useful only after the measurements and model can recognize both success and failure. Each block below removes one reason a large sweep could mislead us.

  1. Verify measurementsReplace proxy scores with width and thickness checks. Add continuity, topology, and material-survival checks.
  2. Reproduce failureMake the centerline void and destructive CMP behavior repeat on controlled geometries.
  3. Check the passing controlUse a prescribed positive control to verify that the geometry and measurements can register the intended state.
  4. Screen the mechanismTest whether suppressor transport points in the direction required for bottom-up Cu growth.
  5. Expand one justified edgeThe next halving improves the response only slightly and survives the reflection check, but still fails every stream.
  6. Check the cylindrical viaUse matched 3D transport to decide whether this law earns morphology work or must be replaced.

Decision rule: a completed batch is not progress by itself. A block advances only after its hard gates and numerical checks pass. Repeated streams and the boundary test must also pass.

EARLIER METHOD

How the first campaign chose experiments

Each result chose the next experiment. Random runs were repeated, downstream recipes shared the same etched shape, and edge results triggered a wider range.

1. List the settingsFind every input the current workflow can change.
2. Search broadlyTry many combinations across wide ranges, with four repeats each.
3. Compare fairlyGive downstream recipes the same etched via so random shape differences do not bias the result.
4. Test the edgesExpand a range when its best result sits at the minimum or maximum.
5. Repeat the bestUse fresh stochastic etched shapes before recommending a recipe; phase one did not control simulator seeds.

Select an experiment to see the question it answered. The internal loss chart was removed because scores from different search ranges were not directly comparable.

StepOld targetOld scorer output

Why the Cu conclusion is suspended

The legacy scalar tracked center height above the floor, not sealed-void topology. A raw 3D topology metric and a coverage/transport fill model are required.

Why the CMP conclusion is suspended

The old one-rate removal could erase Cu and oxide. It could also erase silicon and mask. The new study measures endpoint, overpolish, and selectivity separately. It also measures dish, erosion, and material survival.

No fab recipe is recommended. The selected screening geometry is a test fixture for later 3D transport, physical-model, and process-window work.

HISTORY

What the earlier studies changed

Earlier studies found three mistakes: comparing different depths, trusting one random run, and calling an edge setting optimal before widening the range. The nine experiments above apply those corrections.

WORKFLOW

A measure-run-update workflow

Every step needs a target and a measurement before its output moves downstream.

1. Define successChoose the target and the measurement that can detect failure.
2. Measure each handoffCheck opening CD, etch depth, layer coverage, fill voids, and polish clearance.
3. Test both outcomesUse one known failure and one prescribed passing control.
4. Carry geometry forwardGive downstream comparisons the same upstream shape.
5. Repeat and mapControl random seeds. Then map where results change from pass to fail.
6. Update the modelChange the model when more tuning cannot represent the needed physics.

CD means critical dimension. For the etched via, measure it at the top, middle, and bottom.
