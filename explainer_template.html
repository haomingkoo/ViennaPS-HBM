Using ViennaPS to research a complete TSV traveler

Follow an ongoing investigation from mask opening to CMP. Each experiment shows the question, measured result, interpretation, and the process move it supports. The original 1,948 simulations remain visible as research history; a new foundation audit now controls the conclusions.

The inputs are ViennaPS model coefficients, not wafer-qualified tool recipes. The study covers single-via geometry; it does not cover package warpage, bonding reliability, or signaling.

Current assumptions: one isolated, fully wetted, clean via with uniform local process conditions. Wafer loading, seed resistance, complete plating chemistry, adhesion, film stress, and CMP pad mechanics are not yet represented; each is named where it limits a conclusion.

FOUNDATION CHECK COMPLETE

Use the verified full-width setup before the broad Bosch screen

Observation
All 24 cases are independently valid. Pattern gates pass 24/24; etch mask, CD, depth, and bow pass 24/24, 23/24, 22/24, and 22/24.
Interpretation
The quarter-width shortcut changes depth and top CD; the coarse grid changes six outputs. Mask loss is monotonic, but every tested erosion arm survives.
Process move
Keep the full-width fine grid, qualify the lower ray count, extend erosion until failure appears, and save the selected shapes natively.
MEASUREMENT QUALIFIED

The baseline films are continuous, but not functional

Observation
All 12 layer runs remain continuous and leave the aperture open. The baseline still passes 0/4 liner gates and 0/4 barrier/seed gates at the fine setting.
Interpretation
The liner has enough local thickness but poor deep coverage: lower-wall/field is 0.859 against a 0.995 target. Barrier plus seed is too thin and highly directional: stack minimum is 0.00480 against 0.012.
Process move
Screen lower liner sticking for deeper coverage. For barrier and seed, test more conformal transport and additional dose while protecting the Cu-fill aperture.
SUCCESSFUL SHAPE CONTROL

The model geometry can contain a void-free Cu plug

Observation
The candidate rate law pinches laterally and fails at both tested grids. A prescribed bottom-up control fills the same protected stack at both grids with positive overburden.
Interpretation
The failure is not proof that the via shape is unfillable. It isolates the problem to the growth law: the candidate begins near equal floor and wall velocity when roughly a tenfold floor advantage is required.
Process move
Establish bottom selectivity before sweeping total Cu thickness. More conformal growth alone closes the sidewalls and traps a void.
2D BOUNDARY CLOSED

Lower sticking helps, but the gain is flattening

Observation
At sticking 0.00625, all 24 new runs are valid and the 1,600- and 3,200-reflection results are identical. Suppressor transport improves in all eight paired streams, but the combined gate still passes 0/8.
Interpretation
The worst flux gain is only 0.0041, below the 0.0167 numerical envelope. Realized floor/fastest-wall growth is 1.004–1.011; the depth-to-half-aperture geometry requires about 10.46 for the target-depth via and 25.10 for the deeper case.
Process move
Run the same transport question in a cylindrical 3D via. If 3D also misses, replace or extend the growth law before spending compute on Cu morphology or a large recipe sweep.
PRE-DOE CONTROL READY

CMP can now detect a cut film instead of a disappearing picture

Observation
The new metric checks whether each material region connects the via floor to both mouth sides. Controlled planarization preserves SiO2 stop, barrier, seed, and Cu at two grids.
Interpretation
We can distinguish a connected layer from a cut layer. The removal model is still height and material dependent only; it has no pad-pressure physics.
Process move
Declare allowable stop loss and Cu-plug loss, then screen material selectivity, endpoint, and overpolish. Do not optimize surface recess alone.

EXPERIMENT LOGIC

Why we are not launching the large recipe DOE yet

A broad response surface is useful only after the measurements and model can recognize both success and failure. Each block below removes one reason a large sweep could mislead us.

  1. Verify measurementsReplace proxy scores with CD, thickness, continuity, topology, and material-survival checks.
  2. Reproduce failureMake the centerline void and destructive CMP behavior repeat on controlled geometries.
  3. Prove success is representableUse prescribed positive controls so a bad law is not confused with an impossible geometry.
  4. Screen the mechanismTest whether suppressor transport points in the direction required for bottom-up Cu growth.
  5. Expand one justified edgeThe next halving improves the response only slightly and survives the reflection check, but still fails every stream.
  6. Check the cylindrical viaUse matched 3D transport to decide whether this law earns morphology work or must be replaced.

Decision rule: a completed batch is not progress by itself. A block advances only when its result survives the relevant hard gates, numerical checks, repeated streams, and boundary test.

EARLIER METHOD

Why the first campaign used nine experiments instead of one sweep

We searched broadly first, then used each result to choose the next experiment. Random runs were repeated, recipe comparisons started from the same etched shape, and any best setting at the edge of a range was tested beyond that edge.

1. List the settingsFind every input the current workflow can change.
2. Search broadlyTry many combinations across wide ranges, with four repeats each.
3. Compare fairlyGive downstream recipes the same etched via so random shape differences do not bias the result.
4. Test the edgesExpand a range when its best result sits at the minimum or maximum.
5. Repeat the bestUse fresh stochastic etched shapes before recommending a recipe; phase one did not control simulator seeds.

Select an experiment to see the question it answered. The internal loss chart was removed because scores from different search ranges were not directly comparable.

Why the Cu conclusion is suspended

The legacy scalar tracked center height above the floor, not sealed-void topology. A raw 3D topology metric and a coverage/transport fill model are required.

Why the CMP conclusion is suspended

The old one-rate removal could erase Cu, oxide, silicon, and mask together. The new study separates endpoint, overpolish, selectivity, dish, erosion, and material survival.

No recipe is currently recommended. The active acceptance criterion is a 3D-confirmed, void-free Cu plug with positive overburden followed by field-clear CMP with the protected stack intact.

LEARNINGS

Five rules worth carrying into the next study

Carry the shape forwardA small DRIE change can alter liner coverage, Cu fill, and CMP.
Compare the same viaUse the same etched shape when testing downstream recipes.
Respect failure rulesA lower dish is not useful if CMP destroys the mask.
Test beyond an edgeIf the best setting is the largest or smallest tested, expand the range.
Know when to change modelsMore runs cannot recover physics that the simulator does not contain.

Practical use: narrow physical experiments, identify sensitive recipe families, and catch recipes that win through a scoring artifact. Calibration data is still required before transferring settings to a fab.

HISTORY

What the earlier studies changed

Earlier etch and fill studies exposed three mistakes: comparing etches at different depths, trusting one random run, and calling an edge setting optimal before expanding the range. The nine experiments above apply those corrections throughout.
